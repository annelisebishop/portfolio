The Problem

Apple's internal tools are used by tens of thousands of employees daily across the globe but leadership lacked a consistent way to know whether any of them actually worked for the people using them. Feedback was scattered across support tickets and hallway conversations.

My Role

Program Lead, UX & Research — brought in to build this program from the ground up. Led a cross-functional team spanning data science, engineering, design, and UX research. Owned the full stack: research methodology, in-product feedback UX, data pipeline, executive-facing reporting, and the program roadmap that turned raw sentiment into product decisions.

What I Built

  • A prioritization framework that focused investment on ~15 high-impact products
  • A feedback taxonomy (active vs. passive × high vs. low urgency) to route every signal correctly
  • Two complementary mechanisms: passive always-on capture and structured CSAT campaigns
  • A redesigned intake experience — one clear, consistent flow across all priority products
  • Dual-audience dashboards: granular detail for product teams, portfolio health for executives
  • A recommendation to retire a legacy feature contributing a shrinking share of usable signal — backed by data

Defining Insight

"Don't waste my time" was the clearest user signal that shaped the whole design. People didn't want five different feedback forms across five tools. They wanted one clear way to be heard, routed to the right place.

The Problem

Inside a large internal-technology organization, design existed — but nobody could say how much, where, or to what standard. No common role definitions, no shared leveling, no consistent way to tell whether a team had dedicated design support at all. As the org pushed toward a product-driven model and AI began reshaping what design work looked like, that invisibility became a real risk: you can't invest in, staff, or defend a practice you can't see.

My Approach

Gathered existing org data then conducted 15 in-depth cross-functional interviews across teams with embedded designers, project-based support, and no design at all — plus engineers, product managers, and business stakeholders.

Frameworks That Outlived the Report

  • Engagement model 2×2: Guide / Consult / Partner / Join — gave the org shared vocabulary for how much design investment a team actually needed, and why "one size fits all" wasn't working
  • Outputs vs. outcomes 2×2: gave leadership a fast way to diagnose why some teams got dramatically more value from the same design capacity
  • Both frameworks became the vocabulary for staffing conversations well after the 90 days ended

Impact

"The relationship with design is the best it's ever been." — an engineering leader, describing the shift after design's value became visible and legible for the first time.

The Problem

When I joined Financial Engines as head of brand, the team was well-regarded for craft but treated as a service group — executing what the business had already decided, not shaping what it decided. Growth had stalled. The company had built success on automated portfolio management, but customers increasingly wanted a personal connection alongside a financial plan, not just an algorithm.

My Role

Senior Director, Brand — led a 6–10 person design & content team. Owned research, brand strategy, product design, culture change programming, and the full corporate rebrand rollout across digital, print, and 125 physical advisor centers acquired in a corporate deal.

What I Did

  • Led in-home design research — sat with people in their living rooms and kitchens to understand how they actually thought about their financial lives
  • Paired qualitative work with a large-scale quantitative segmentation survey to define new positioning
  • Designed the full end-to-end customer experience for a new personal financial-advisor offer
  • Treated internal culture change as a design problem: launched a recurring speaker series (the Speakeasy), brought real customers to the company all-hands, and designed large-scale office installations
  • Managed the full rebrand rollout across 125 acquired advisor centers on a budget-constrained timeline

Defining Insight

What made us distinctive wasn't what we did — competitors could say the same things. What made us distinctive was who we served and how we created value for them.
